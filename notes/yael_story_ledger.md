# Story Ledger — *Yael* (working title)

An inward-looking literary work. Most events take place in the protagonist's mind. Fable register, light on technical mechanism, in the vein of *Never Let Me Go* — an alternate reality where the science is atmosphere, not subject.

---

## 1. Premise (settled)

In a near-future / alternate present, human reproductive cloning exists as a mature clinical procedure. It is treated, where legal, as a **third routine option** alongside IVF and surrogacy — not as transgression but as ordinary medicine. Couples use it to have a child of their own genetics, or to recover a lost or deceased child.

- **Germany:** cloning prohibited. Yael grows up without cloned peers, without a community that shares her condition — a chosen isolation that feeds the interior, lonely texture of the book.
- **China (and elsewhere):** cloning permitted and routine. Yael *could* have found peers had she been raised there. This road-not-taken matters: her sense of singularity is partly an accident of geography.

---

## 2. Characters

### Ilam — the father
- A **physician**. This is load-bearing, not incidental:
  - He understood the biology and secured genetic material himself.
  - He arranged the procedure discreetly with Chinese providers.
  - Crucially, he grasps intellectually that a clone is a **separate person**. He does **not** see his dead wife in Yael. He raises her as a daughter, never as a surrogate-partner or a resurrection. His clarity here is his dignity — and also, later, part of what wounds Yael, because his very rationality can read as coldness.
- He **always intended to tell her.** The disclosure was planned from the start, timed to her coming of age. It was never a slip or a cornering.

### Yael — the protagonist (clone of the late wife)
- Genetically identical to Ilam's deceased first wife. Female (donor was female — consistent).
- Raised as Ilam's daughter, unaware of her origin until 18.
- Carries a **bodily tell**: a propensity to infections, unexplained through childhood, later understood as a residue of the cloning process. This seeds an early, wordless unease ("something in me runs differently") that pays off after the reveal.

### The late wife — the donor
- Died in an accident before having children with Ilam.
- Present throughout as absence: photographs, Ilam's grief, and — unbearably — as Yael's own face. Decide how much Yael eventually wants to know her.

### The surrogate — "temporary mother"
- Carried and delivered Yael in China. Currently a plot device.
- **Open question (see below):** does Yael seek her out? She is a natural destination for the identity quest.

### Ilam's brother
- Once noticed Yael's resemblance to the late wife.
- Was told she was **one of several IVF embryos prepared before the wife's death** — a cover story that *cannot be disproven*. This is how the 18-year silence holds without straining belief.

---

## 3. How the secret held for 18 years (settled)

- The practical work was done in China; no one in Ilam's circle witnessed it.
- The IVF-embryo lie neutralizes the one person (the brother) who noticed.
- In Germany, no cloned peers, no clinic records within reach, few who knew the wife well.
- Photographs of the wife were likely put away — itself a quiet grief-detail worth planting.

---

## 4. Framing corrections (settled)

- **Not "buying a person."** Reframed: cloning is a normalized medical procedure, cousin to IVF and surrogacy. The commodification anxiety, if present, lives in *Yael's* interpretation after the fact, not in the neutral mechanics of the world. She may *feel* ordered-into-being even though the world doesn't see it that way — that gap is thematic.
- **No resurrection fantasy.** Ilam's physician's clarity forecloses the gothic "he remade his wife" reading. The story is not about a man loving a copy; it's about a person discovering she *is* one.

---

## 5. The trigger — OPEN, needs strengthening

The "now I can have my own children" quip is retained as **pretext, not cause** — Ilam had always planned to tell her around 18, and the joke merely gives him his opening. That resolves the "too convenient" worry in principle.

**Still to decide:** whether to add a second, quieter pressure that makes *this* the moment — e.g. Yael about to take a DNA ancestry test, an old photo surfacing, a medical appointment about her infections. Even if the quip stays the visible trigger, an underlying pressure deepens the inevitability. *Left open pending your call.*

---

## 6. The bodily tell (settled, with a barb)

Yael's proneness to infection is later linked to the cloning process. The barb: she **blames Ilam** for not having paid for the best available service — a brilliant, ugly, human accusation that turns her body into evidence of his choices, and love into a transaction after all. Keep this; it's one of the sharpest beats.

---

## 7. Structure / register (direction)

- Inward-looking; much happens in Yael's mind.
- Consider withholding the cloning fact from the **reader** only briefly, then giving the whole back half to Yael metabolizing it: memory, projection, imagined dialogue with the dead woman she *is*, the vertigo of being a copy who must become an original.
- Governing question: **what makes a person fully her own?** Explored through interiority, not plot machinery.

---

## Open questions (for later passes)

1. Does the quip stay the sole trigger, or add an underlying pressure (test / photo / diagnosis)?
2. Does Yael seek out the surrogate? The late wife's family / history?
3. How much does Yael want to *know* the woman whose face she wears — and does Ilam help or resist?
4. How is the brother's cover story handled once Yael learns the truth — does she confront the lie's architecture?
5. How visible is the alternate-reality worldbuilding? (Recommend: minimal, ambient.)
