# Story Ledger — *Shoresh* (שורש)

**Title: settled — *Shoresh* / שורש** ("root"). Chosen over *My Roots*, *Being a Crime*, *Neither Sister Nor Daughter*, and *Parentlessness*.

The father is **Ilan** (אילן, "tree") — permanently renamed from the earlier *Ilam*, which carried none of the arboreal meaning the title architecture depends on. **Locked for the long run.**

The **protagonist is Nitzanah** (ניצנה, *blossom / flower bud*) — **settled.** Chosen over *Nurit* (נורית, buttercup) and the original running name *Yael* (יעל, ibex). *Nitzanah* closes the botanical triad the book is built on — **root → tree → bud** (origin → parent → unfolding new growth) — and the bud, as the pure not-yet-become, is the truest image for a copy who must become an original. *Yael* named an animal and always sat outside that scheme; *Nurit* fit the plant world but named a finished bloom rather than a becoming. Transliteration *Nitzanah* (final-h) per the author's prologue.

An inward-looking literary work. Most events take place in the protagonist's mind. Fable register, light on technical mechanism, in the vein of *Never Let Me Go* — an alternate reality where the science is atmosphere, not subject.

---

## 1. Premise (settled)

In a near-future / alternate present, human reproductive cloning exists as a mature clinical procedure. It is treated, where legal, as a **third routine option** alongside IVF and surrogacy — not as transgression but as ordinary medicine. Couples use it to have a child of their own genetics, or to recover a lost or deceased child.

- **Germany:** cloning prohibited. Nitzanah grows up without cloned peers, without a community that shares her condition — a chosen isolation that feeds the interior, lonely texture of the book.
- **China (and elsewhere):** cloning permitted and routine. Nitzanah *could* have found peers had she been raised there. This road-not-taken matters: her sense of singularity is partly an accident of geography.

---

## 2. Characters

### Ilan — the father
- A **physician**. This is load-bearing, not incidental:
  - He understood the biology and secured genetic material himself.
  - He arranged the procedure discreetly with Chinese providers.
  - Crucially, he grasps intellectually that a clone is a **separate person**. He does **not** see his dead wife in Nitzanah. He raises her as a daughter, never as a surrogate-partner or a resurrection. His clarity here is his dignity — and also, later, part of what wounds Nitzanah, because his very rationality can read as coldness.
- He **always intended to tell her.** The disclosure was planned from the start, timed to her coming of age. It was never a slip or a cornering.

### Nitzanah — the protagonist (clone of the late wife)
- Genetically identical to Ilan's deceased first wife. Female (donor was female — consistent).
- Raised as Ilan's daughter, unaware of her origin until 18.
- Carries a **bodily tell**: a propensity to infections, unexplained through childhood, later understood as a residue of the cloning process. This seeds an early, wordless unease ("something in me runs differently") that pays off after the reveal.

### The late wife — the donor
- Died in an accident before having children with Ilan.
- Present throughout as absence: photographs, Ilan's grief, and — unbearably — as Nitzanah's own face. Decide how much Nitzanah eventually wants to know her.

### The surrogate — "temporary mother"
- Carried and delivered Nitzanah in China. Currently a plot device.
- **Open question (see below):** does Nitzanah seek her out? She is a natural destination for the identity quest.

### Ilan's brother
- Once noticed Nitzanah's resemblance to the late wife.
- Was told she was **one of several IVF embryos prepared before the wife's death** — a cover story that *cannot be disproven*. This is how the 18-year silence holds without straining belief.

## 3. How the secret held for 18 years (settled)

- The practical work was done in China; no one in Ilan's circle witnessed it.
- The IVF-embryo lie neutralizes the one person (the brother) who noticed.
- In Germany, no cloned peers, no clinic records within reach, few who knew the wife well.
- Photographs of the wife were likely put away — itself a quiet grief-detail worth planting.

## 4. Framing corrections (settled)

- **Not "buying a person."** Reframed: cloning is a normalized medical procedure, cousin to IVF and surrogacy. The commodification anxiety, if present, lives in *Nitzanah's* interpretation after the fact, not in the neutral mechanics of the world. She may *feel* ordered-into-being even though the world doesn't see it that way — that gap is thematic.
- **No resurrection fantasy.** Ilan's physician's clarity forecloses the gothic "he remade his wife" reading. The story is not about a man loving a copy; it's about a person discovering she *is* one.

## 7. Structure / register (direction)

- Inward-looking; much happens in Nitzanah's mind.
- Consider withholding the cloning fact from the **reader** only briefly, then giving the whole back half to Nitzanah metabolizing it: memory, projection, imagined dialogue with the dead woman she *is*, the vertigo of being a copy who must become an original.
- Governing question: **what makes a person fully her own?** Explored through interiority, not plot machinery.
- **Narration (aim, to experiment with):** alternate POV chapter by chapter — Ilan / Nitzanah. Both interiorities get airtime; Ch.1 (*Start*) is Ilan's. So the "back half in Nitzanah's mind" above becomes a *rhythm*, not a single handover.
- **Tense:** present throughout (the POV character's present). Watch for slips.
- **Texture:** see §13 for the description philosophy (spare but grounded; withhold the world; hoard the face for the mirror).
- **Character-register contrast (intentional, load-bearing).** The two POVs use *different conventions*: Nitzanah = interior, present-immediate, **unmarked/internalized dialogue**; Ilan = external, retrospective, clause-heavy, **quoted/reported dialogue**, and he copes/loves through *action and provision* rather than introspection. **Purpose:** to *enact* through form — not state — that Nitzanah is a full subject, so the question "is she the mother?" never even arises. **Guardrails so it reads as two people, not two rules:** (a) keep it a *tendency, not a law* — let each voice contaminate the other under pressure (Ilan's externality cracking inward *via action*, e.g. the Ch.5 tea ceremony; Nitzanah earning outward-looking moments as she grows); (b) spread the difference across *several* axes (rhythm, sentence length, relation to time, humour, what each notices) so "inward vs. outward" isn't the only marker; (c) handle the dialogue convention — the most *nameable* marker — with restraint (line breaks; reserve unmarked for her *most* internalized moments, not all her speech). Ties Q11 (dialogue convention) and Q12 (resemblance). Don't over-sand it either: the distinct textures are a real strength — aim for contrast that reads as two people, not a muted middle.

## 8. Title — *Shoresh* / שורש (settled)

Chosen for a density no English option matched:

- **Literal:** *root* — origins, lineage, where a self comes from.
- **Grammatical:** in Hebrew, the *shoresh* is the consonantal root from which every conjugated word-form derives — many surface words, one underlying root. Nitzanah's exact condition: same root as the wife, a different word.
- **Arboreal echo:** if the father's name plays on *ilan* (tree), the pairing tree/root binds the visible growth to the hidden origin it cannot acknowledge.

**Watch-item:** opacity for non-Hebrew readers. **Now actioned by the prologue (§9)**, which glosses the word up front so the title reads as earned rather than imported mystique.

**Runners-up (for the record):** *Neither Sister Nor Daughter* — the accessible alternative; names the collapse of kinship categories, with its power in the unspoken third it refuses to say (*wife*). Rejected as slightly long and prone to a first-read hunt for the "sister" referent. *Being a Crime* and *Parentlessness* rejected for fighting the settled world (transgression is dialed down; Nitzanah is not parentless) and for over-promising a louder, more external book.

## 9. Prologue — lexicon entries (settled)

**Device:** the book opens with dictionary-style entries — no narrative, just definitions — for שורש (*shoresh*, root), אילן (*ilan*, tree / a male given name), and ניצנה (*Nitzanah*, blossom / flower bud / a female given name). The three headwords are the three principals and the three stages of one plant: **root → tree → bud**.

**Why it works here:**
- **Pre-empts the title's one liability.** Glossing *shoresh* up front dissolves the opacity flagged in §8; the word arrives earned.
- **Plants tree/root wordlessly.** By the time Ilan appears, the resonance is already in the reader's ear.
- **The polysemy is native, not manufactured.** שורש genuinely bears the botanical, linguistic, and mathematical senses in real Hebrew usage — the layering points at meanings that are already there.

**Two buried lines to protect (do not nudge the reader toward either):**
1. **"vegetative reproduction"** inside the botanical definition — asexual propagation, a plant generating a genetically identical copy of itself. This *is* cloning, stated in the vocabulary of roots, and it's botanically accurate. Subliminal on first read; detonates on reread. Keep it exactly where it sits.
2. **The linguistic root** — many surface words conjugated from one consonantal root is precisely Nitzanah's condition: same shoresh as the wife, a different word-form.

**Square-root sense — resolved:** **kept.** All four senses of *shoresh* remain in the final prologue, including the mathematical (√x as a value that reproduces x through self-multiplication — identity via doubling).

**Discipline:** three entries — root, tree, bud — form a closed triad and are the ceiling; resist expanding into a full glossary.

**Third-entry question — resolved:** the entry is **added, not withheld.** The prologue defines all three — *shoresh / Ilan / Nitzanah* — so the triad **root → tree → bud** stands as the whole story in three dictionary lines: origin, parent, unfinished new growth. (The withholding alternative — leaving the protagonist undefined among root and tree — belonged to the discarded *Yael* option and is now moot.)

**PIVOTAL OPEN QUESTION (Q10) — what is "the news," and does Nitzanah now know she's a clone?** Ambiguous by design, but the ledger needs the underlying fact for downstream consistency. Readings:
- **(A) A real medical diagnosis.** The screening — set up as mere cover — actually turned up a serious condition in Nitzanah (plausibly a cloning consequence; ties hard to §6). ## 13. Texture / description philosophy (governing rule — peer to §7)

**Frame.** The question is never "more or less description" but **interiority-bearing description vs. inventory**. In an inward book the second deadens; the first is fuel. Aim: **spare but grounded** — *placed* description, not more of it. One exact, load-bearing detail where the temptation is five. The current compression is right; hold it.

**By category:**
- **The world / near-future — withhold.** Keep it millimetres thick (an apartment, a clinic, a taxi). No rendering of the cloning industry, the tech, the altered society — that trades the fable for expository SF and evaporates the power of a girl who is her dead mother inside a mundane Germany. The reader should almost forget it's the future. Strangeness lives in the *situation*, not the set. (*Never Let Me Go* touchstone; ties to Q5.)
- **Nitzanah's face / looks — HOARD, do not spend early.** Her face is the dead mother's — the single most charged fact in the book, a weapon, not a texture. Describing it early either pre-detonates the resemblance or (if via Ilan) breaks the §2 rule that he does *not* see the wife in her. **Deliver it at full force *after* the reveal, through a charged object** — a mirror, or one of the mother's put-away photographs — ideally in a Nitzanah chapter where her lifelong reflection suddenly *means* something. First-person POV enforces this (narrators don't catalog their own faces); use the constraint. **(Depends on Q12: this payoff requires Nitzanah to objectively resemble the mother — see the Ch.5 resemblance flag.)**
- **Bodies generally — gesture > portrait.** One anchoring detail each, max; motion and habit over features. (Already working: Ilan behind the armrests, Nitzanah exhaling to prove no smoke.)
- **Apartment — yes, selectively, as setting-as-grief.** The one place to materialize the dead mother: her things, or their conspicuous *absence* — a photo turned face-down, a chair no one sits in. Every detail filtered through the POV character's state, carrying mood/meaning, never furnishing.
- **Clinic — the inverse.** Cold, under-furnished, sterile impersonality as counterpoint to the devastation. (Model already present: Nitzanah's "the nurses look different… they *are* different" = unease wearing a clinic's clothes.)

**Failure mode to watch.** Extreme compression → the reader floats; emotional payoffs need a body and a place to land. A couple of current spots sit near that edge. **Test point:** the end of Ch.3 ("the most dreaded moment: to be alone with her") wants *one* domestic detail on entry — the thing Ilan's eye snags on — to give the dread a room to happen in.

**One-line rule:** withhold the world; hoard the face for the mirror; let grief-loaded objects into the apartment.

## 15. Comparables & positioning (reference — read before drafting outward)

**The through-line (your ground).** Across every comparable found, East and West, the copy is almost always one of three things: a **memory-continuation** (the dead literally returned), a **griever's projection / echo**, or an **instrumentalized replacement**. *Shoresh*'s distinguishing move is to be **none** of these: Nitzanah is a genuinely autonomous **new person**, raised as a **daughter (not a resurrection)**, narrated from **her own interiority**, and Ilan's dignity is precisely his **refusal to make her a replacement**. Protect this above all.

**The two engines to keep switched OFF (the signature):**
1. the **romance / incest** engine — the driver of *Womb*, the frame of *Solaris*;
2. the **memory-transfer "they're really back"** engine — *Replicas*, *Swan Song*, *The Clone Returns Home*.
Refusing both is what separates you from the whole field.

**Nearest neighbours — study, do not echo:**
- **Womb** (2010 film, Fliegauf) — the near-twin: near-future normalized cloning, clone a dead lover and raise them, secret origins, late reveal, hushed tone. It even contains your exact premise as a throwaway slur — "artificial incest," a girl raised as the clone of a dead female relative. Avoid its incest engine and creator-only POV. **Don't read as "Womb, novelized."**
- **Solaris** (Lem, 1961 novel; Tarkovsky, 1972 film; Soderbergh, 2002) — the ur-question: a copy of the dead wife (Hari), built from the griever's memory ("the materialization of your conception of her"), self-aware, is-she-a-person. *Shoresh* rotates it 180°: the copy's POV, no projection, no romance, a new self to build.
- **A Number** (Churchill, 2002 play) — closest literary relative: a father, a cloned child, the "you are a clone" reveal, guilt, nature/nurture, terse economy. Differentiate via dead-wife-as-daughter, sustained interiority, and a single autonomous clone (not "a number").
- **Neon Genesis Evangelion** (1995) — closest East Asian: Rei, a clone of the protagonist's dead mother (Yui); identity / replaceability; even the §6 bodily-fragility echo (unstable genetics, constant medication). Differentiate: no cosmic machinery, a single person, and a father who **refuses** to instrumentalize her toward the dead wife.
- **Ishiguro, twice** — *Never Let Me Go* (your touchstone; clones-as-donors, the opposite thesis) and *Klara and the Sun* (replace a dying child with a copy, from the copy's POV — but AI, and a servant's-eye view). Same author as your reference; stay conscious of the resemblance.

**The field, grouped:**
- *Bullseye — clone a specific dead loved one, live with the copy:* Womb; Replicas; Swan Song; **The Echo Wife** (your inverse — the husband makes the clone a **wife**, the move Ilan refuses).
- *Literary clone-identity / the reveal:* A Number; Never Let Me Go; The House of the Scorpion; Cloud Atlas (Sonmi); Moon; Orphan Black.
- *Copy-of-the-dead via projection or AI:* Solaris; Klara and the Sun; Black Mirror "Be Right Back"; Marjorie Prime; After Yang.
- *East Asian:* Evangelion (Rei); The Clone Returns Home (Nakajima, 2008); cousins — Seobok (KR), Air Doll (Kore-eda).
- *Ancestors:* Frankenstein (made being + creator's responsibility → maps onto §6); Brave New World (cloning as dehumanization); The Boys from Brazil (clones raised in families; nature/nurture).

**Contemporary resonance (a positioning asset).** The premise is a live nerve, not a dated conceit: AI "griefbots" now sell replicas of the dead (doc *Eternal You*, 2024), and grief-driven cloning is real (the cloning scientist in *King of Clones*, 2023: "grief is the catalyst"; Streisand's cloned dog). The fable taps something people are actually reaching for.

**Verdict.** The *premise* is not original — the field is dense and includes a near-twin (Womb), a close literary relative (A Number), the genre's ur-text (Solaris), and your own touchstone author twice (Ishiguro). But "done better" doesn't apply: the well-made neighbours do **different** things, and the ones sharing your premise were mostly pulp, wrong-POV, or taboo-forward. The open, unworked gap is exactly yours — the **quiet, interior, taboo-refusing, daughter-not-replacement** version, with its Hebrew / botanical architecture. **Risk:** being read as derivative of *Womb* or *Solaris*. **Mitigation:** lean into the two refusals above.
