# STORY LEDGER — *[Untitled — the Clone Project novel]*
**Version 0.6** · working canon for continuity

> This is the running record of settled facts ("canon"), decisions still open, and consistency risks to watch. Add to it as we build. Where an entry is my inference rather than your explicit decision, it's tagged **(proposed)** — approve, change, or cut.

---

## 1. Logline
Eighteen years after a grieving state secretly cloned its fallen elite, one of the clone-daughters is quietly reclassified as worthless and sent to disappear — not to discard her, but to hide her. When her handler is taken to draw her out, she has to learn who she was grown from, why her body is a key, and how to live once she knows.

---

## 2. Timeline & Setting
- **Cloning is technically feasible since ~1996 (Dolly).** In this world the barrier to human cloning has never been capability — it's ethics and law. A near-universal taboo (treaty + domestic prohibition) keeps it undone.
- **Trigger:** the aftermath of **October 7, 2023**. The state breaks the taboo and launches a clandestine cloning program.
- **Story present: ~2042** (Yael is 18; program embryos created ~2024).
- Consequence to hold onto: because the program is illegal under international norms, the state **cannot act loudly**. No open manhunts. This constrains every antagonist's move for the whole book.

---

## 3. The Program
- Grows clones of **fallen elite personnel** killed on/around Oct 7.
- **Yael's line is the exception (⚠ ripple, see §4 → Original).** Her donor was *severely wounded* on Oct 7 and **survived**; samples were taken "just in case" she died and **used anyway**. Keep this a **bureaucratic anomaly** (samples banked, never reconciled against her recovery), *not* a targeted plot — that protects "coincidence, not design," and the memorial premise still holds for every *other* line.
- **Sisters raised entirely apart**; they have never met and are kept unaware of / uncontactable to each other.
- The state **always knew what each clone is** and raised them deliberately — not as ordinary citizens.
- **Separation is standard compartmentalization** (security — clones don't know each other), *not* a hunt for the special one. Nobody grew the sisters apart *expecting* a key; Yael's convergence surfaces later, via routine developmental data and/or the Agency's targeted tracking once it needs the trove (see §6, Q2).
- **Each line gets handlers matched to the original's domain.** A soldier-origin line draws soldier handlers; an **intelligence-origin line (Yael's) draws intelligence handlers** — which is why Ilam is a spook (§4). Side effect worth using: the Program reconstitutes not just the person but her *milieu* — each line is a small pocket of the shoresh's world.

## 4. Characters

### Yael — protagonist
- One of the cloned daughters; **18** at story open.
- **Educated and trained inside the program**, with real skills beyond civilian schooling. She is competent — this is established, not something she has to acquire in act two.
- At the start she does **not** know who her original was.

### Ilam — her handler *(he/him)*
- **Intelligence by background — because the *shoresh* was.** Per the line-handler rule (§3), an intelligence-origin line draws an intelligence handler. Ilam is that man: a spook serving as the **Program's line-handler** for Yael's sisters. (He sits at a seam — intelligence by *background*, Program by *employment*; see §5.)
- **His authority to reclassify her** comes from that line-handler role (answers much of Q4); the Program's wind-down (§5) is the cover that lets a quiet downgrade pass.
- **What he told Yael — Q1, resolved: only the basics.** That she has many sisters living elsewhere; that they might meet someday; that her *shoresh* was "special," which is why the line was chosen. He did **not** reveal the shoresh's identity, that her domain was intelligence, the trove, or that Yael herself is the key. *(This also retires the old "how does Yael even know she has sisters" gap — Ilam told her.)*
- **What he knew:** the shoresh was intelligence-special (baseline, from his own posting) — but **nothing special about Yael specifically until the Agency began tracking all her sisters** (§5–6). Being a pro, he read the tracking as danger and moved, **without necessarily knowing the mechanism** (the epigenetic lock). **(proposed) Dramatic irony:** he hid his ward out of general protectiveness, not knowing she was the one clone the Agency most needs — which is exactly why they tear the apartment apart and take *him*.
- His reclassification to "3rd-class / farming / numeric" is that protective move — make her look worthless so the search passes over her.
- Taken captive — **kept alive on purpose:** his value is as the handler who knows Yael intimately and can predict her. Gives him agency in captivity (resist, mislead, stall).

### The sisters
- Multiple clones of the same shoresh; genetically identical to Yael.
- **Reason they exist = the program's whole reason:** to field a new generation of *proven-competent warriors*. The sisters are the program **working as intended** — Yael is the anomaly, not them.
- **Yael's predicament is coincidence, not design.** It attaches to her only because (a) her shoresh left a **locked trove** ("closed doors") and is now **beyond reach / locked out of it herself** (§4 → Original, §6), and (b) Yael is the one sister who **converged** and can open it. Two accidents stacked: she is the *only accessible key to the one woman's secrets someone needs*. The other sisters carry neither burden, so they simply proceed as warriors.
- **Dramatic uses (proposed, pick as needed):**
  - *The mirror / the road not taken* — a sister who became exactly what Yael was "supposed" to be: a warrior, not a key.
  - *The warrior-sister as antagonist* — A deploys a competent sister to **retrieve Yael alive** (identical instincts = best tracker; trusted by A). Their meeting would be the **first time Yael ever meets a sister** — she confronts her own face. Ripe for sympathy/defection, since the sister is looking at herself.
- Consistent with canon that the sisters **have never met** — any sister encounter is a first, and a big beat.

### The Original — Yael's word: **Shoresh** (שורש, "root")
- The name **Yael herself finds/chooses** for the woman she was grown from — human, not official. In Hebrew *shoresh* is also the consonantal root every word grows from, so a line of sisters each reads as an inflection of one *shoresh*. (English echo, if ever needed: "root" also = root access — she is the root key to the trove.)
- **Held in reserve** (use if the story bends toward one of these registers):
  - *Makor* (מקור, "source") — also intelligence *source/informant*; the cold, spy-flavored option. Good candidate for what **the institution** calls her (see split below).
  - *Kadmona* (קדמונה, "the primordial one") — mystical, heavy, the first woman.
  - *Rishona* (ראשונה, "the First") — plain and clean.
  - English-only fallbacks: *Source*, *Origin*, *Template* (how the program sees her), *Prime*.
- **(proposed) Official-vs-found split:** let the institution use a cold term (*makor* / a serial code / "the template") and keep **Shoresh** as Yael's human counter-name. The gap between the two is free characterization, and the word can warm as she does.
- **She is ALIVE — the spine's biggest reveal (this is how D1 resolves).** On **Oct 7, 2023** she was **severely wounded, not killed**. Believed to be dying, her **samples were taken "just in case"** (standard for mortally-wounded elite). She **recovered** — but the samples were **used anyway**, producing Yael's line (§3 anomaly). *(Fully fictional — not modeled on an identifiable real casualty.)*
- An **elite intelligence officer.** Her intelligence work is why a valuable **data trove** exists — sealed to her **epigenetic signature** (§6).
- **Reveal structure:** Yael was raised to believe her original *fell* on Oct 7 (cover: "fallen elite soldier"). Learning the woman is **alive** is a major mid/late-book turn — and opens the door to Yael **meeting her original**, a climax the dead version couldn't offer.
- **⚠ New load-bearing question — if she lives, why is Yael needed? (see §6 + D2).** Working answer: the living shoresh is **not a usable key** — her epigenome has **drifted** from the signature she sealed years ago, so she has *locked herself out*; the clone grown from the 2023 sample matches the **archived-era self** better than the woman does now. Optionally reinforce: she's also **out of reach** (vanished / defected / presumed dead). **Her current status and motive are the next thing to pin down (D2).**

## 5. Institutions / Factions
Different arms of the state, different purposes — this is the spine of the conflict.
- **Institution A — "the Agency."** The intelligence service the *shoresh* worked for. Wants the trove; **needs Yael alive** *because the original herself can't serve as the key* — locked out and/or out of reach (§4, §6). Its search takes the visible form of **tracking all the sisters of the line** — hunting the one who can open the closed doors. It raids the apartment and takes Ilam.
- **Institution B — "the Program."** The cloning / clone-management body. Grows and raises the clones and **staffs each line with domain-matched handlers** (§3). Ilam serves *here*, as Yael's line-handler. Its priorities are not A's.
- **Ilam sits at the seam:** intelligence by *background*, Program by *employment*. **(knob)** Is he **on loan from the Agency** (→ hiding Yael makes him a traitor to his own service — higher stakes, richer interrogation) or **retired/independent** intelligence (→ cleaner faction lines)? Pick per how much loyalty-agony you want.
- **(proposed) Third pressure:** the Program is winding down / politically radioactive — the cover under which Ilam can quietly reclassify Yael, and a clock forcing the Agency to grab before the clones scatter.

## 6. The Mechanism — the epigenetic lock *(load-bearing; keep coherent)*
- The sisters share the original's **genome**. What sets Yael apart is her **epigenetic imprinting** — marks shaped by her own **temperament and tastes**, which happen to converge on the original ("her mother") and are **not** shared by the sisters.
- **(proposed) Why a living key, why her:** the trove is sealed to the shoresh's **epigenetic signature**, not her DNA — raw genetic material is useless. Only a clone whose epigenome has **converged** closely enough can regenerate a viable key from her own **living cells**. Yael crossed that threshold — so they need *her*, and *alive*.
- **(proposed) Why not the original herself? (the crux, now she lives):** the lock holds the signature she sealed **years ago**, and **epigenetics drift** (age, the 2023 trauma, time). The present-day shoresh has moved past that self and **no longer matches her own lock** — she has *locked herself out*. Yael, grown from the 2023 sample and convergent, matches the **archived-era self** better than the woman does now. Optionally reinforce with unreachability (D2).
- **(proposed) The mother's design:** she sealed her archive to her own signature as a **trust-no-one-but-myself** measure. She locked her secrets to *who she was* — and only the daughter who *became* that self can open them.
- **(proposed) Why the plot ignites now — need-driven, not fate-driven:** the trigger is that **A now needs what's behind the shoresh's closed doors** (a live intelligence problem, or the program's wind-down forcing a grab before the clones scatter — see §5). Only then does A go looking for a key, test the sisters, and land on Yael. Her convergence is a **pre-existing accident they discover**, not a destiny that activates — which keeps Yael a person caught in machinery, not a chosen one.
- **Why now — ✅ RESOLVED:** two things stack. **(a)** The natural key — the original — is **unusable** (drifted out of her own lock; and/or out of reach, D2). **(b)** The clone-key **only ripens at adulthood**, so **~2042** is the first moment the Agency's last-resort option even exists. Unusable-original + key-now-viable = the Agency moves. *(Optional accelerant: something in the trove turns urgent now. The old death-based orphaning — dead successor, custodian's brain tumor — is retired with D1; keep those beats only as extra "no human backup either" texture if you want.)*

## 7. Inciting Incident & Spine
1. Ilam discloses Yael's nature and reclassifies her — protection disguised as demotion.
2. Yael **flips out and bolts**, reading it as betrayal/abandonment (dramatic irony — the demotion was love). **(confirm this irony is intended — it's strong.)**
3. She returns to find **Ilam gone, the apartment torn apart** (A searching for what Ilam hid about her origin).
4. The book: Yael finds Ilam, learns who the shoresh was, that **she is alive**, and why her own body is the only key — then works out how to live in peace. **Possible climax:** Yael meets her living original.

## 9. Consistency Watchlist
- The state can't act openly (illegal program) — check every antagonist beat obeys this.
- Clones share DNA but not fingerprints/irises — never let a plot point treat clones as biometrically identical except on the epigenetic axis in §6.
- Keep the mother **fictional**; the real Oct 7 event is the setting, not a real individual. (Craft/sensitivity call — yours to make.)
- Ilam is **he/him** throughout.
- "Told her everything" (Q1) must not leak the §4 reveal — "everything" = the basics only.
- **Keep "the Agency" (A) and "the Program" (B) distinct.** Ilam is intelligence by *background* but Program by *employment* — don't let his spook credentials trick the reader into thinking his employer is the Agency hunting Yael (unless you take the on-loan/traitor knob, §5).
- **The shoresh LIVES (D1 resolved).** She was wounded on Oct 7, not killed. But Yael was raised to believe the original *fell* — don't let Yael (or the reader, early) know otherwise before the reveal.
- **The living original must stay UNUSABLE as the key** — locked out by drift and/or out of reach (§6, D2) — or Yael's necessity collapses and the engine stalls. Guard this in every scene that touches the shoresh.
- **Yael's line is a survivor-origin anomaly** — keep it a bureaucratic accident, never a targeted plot, to protect "coincidence, not design."
- Keep the lock **villain-free**: no hidden assassin engineered it — it's her own trust-no-one measure plus chance.
- **Why grow warriors instead of building machines? ✅ RESOLVED as theme, not patch** — see §10. The choice is *civilizational*: a culture built on seed/lineage continues the fallen by blood, and carries the golem as its cautionary tale against man-made servants. (Still fine to seed a concrete in-world military rationale too, but it's no longer load-bearing.)

## 10. Thematic Bedrock — Seed vs. Golem
*(Not decoration — the spine. Also the real answer to "why clones, not machines," see §9.)*

**The civilizational choice.** This world runs on *lineage / seed* (zera), not fabrication. The covenant is a promise about offspring ("seed as the stars"); kingship (David) and priesthood (Aaron) pass by blood; the messianic hope is a *descendant* of David; and identity itself passes **through the mother** (matrilineal). So a state facing near-annihilation reaches instinctively to **continue the fallen by seed** — not to build machines. It is a **technological *yibbum*** (levirate marriage): the Torah commands continuing a dead man's seed "that his name not be blotted out." The program exists to keep the *names* of the fallen alive. *(Magen David: lean on its symbolism as David's shield / the dynastic line — its ancient Davidic pedigree is more legend than documented.)*

**The golem is the road not taken.** The artificial servant — clay animated by an inscribed name, soulless, mute, literal, the protector that turns menace and must be destroyed — is the **golem**, ancestor of the modern robot (the word coined by Čapek from "forced labor," golem-inflected). A culture that authored the cautionary tale about man-made servants distrusts autonomous war-machines. **Machines = clay; clones = seed.**

**The women are the motif made flesh** (the protagonist's name is not incidental):
- **Yael** (Judges 4–5) — ends the enemy general through intimacy and cunning (milk, then the tent-peg), not on the field. "Most blessed of women." The lethal-competent female; an intelligence officer's true métier. *Our protagonist.*
- **Esther** — hidden identity inside hostile power; her name = hiddenness, God's name absent from her book (*hester panim*). The concealed clone-self, the numeric erasure, the undercover shoresh.
- **Ruth** — the outsider whose *chosen* loyalty makes her David's great-grandmother: where inherited and chosen meet. Yael's question — only the seed, or does she choose?
- **Rachel** — weeping for her scattered children, refusing comfort, answered by *they shall return*. The absent shoresh; the dispersed sisters; the ache and hope of gathering them.

**Master motif — name vs. number.** A golem is ruled by a name and *killed by erasing it* (*emet* → *met*, truth → dead). Stripping Yael's name and issuing a **number** is precisely that: the **golemization of a person** — seed turned back into clay, a daughter into an asset, the name *blotted out* that *yibbum* exists to preserve. The book's central atrocity and its central value are **the same act from two sides.** Spine of the story: *are the clones named daughters (souls, a line) or numbered golems (tools)?* The state chose seed to make them; one arm of it now tries to turn them back to clay.

**Ripple from "she lives" — Yael's line sharpens the question.** The general program still *resurrects the fallen* (memorial, *yibbum*, keeping the dead's names alive) — that holds for every other line. But Yael's original **didn't die**, so Yael is not the dead brought back; she is the **living, copied**. A colder, stranger golem-question: not "can we continue a lost name?" but "what are you, when your original still walks around?" Her fight for a *name* over a *number* lands harder when the person she duplicates is alive and un-erased. Use the contrast — the other sisters are monuments; Yael is a **duplicate**.
